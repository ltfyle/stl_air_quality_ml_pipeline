# What you need to know about air pollution
Under the Clean Air Act, the EPA has established National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) for six major pollutants known to impact public health.

## Criteria Pollutants
- Carbon Monoxide  
- Lead  
- Ground-Level Ozone  
- Nitrogen Dioxide  
- Sulfur Dioxide  
- Particle Pollution  

## The Air Quality Index (AQI)

State and local environmental agencies monitor air quality and report their data to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA). The EPA compiles this information into the national Air Quality System (AQS) database.

Pollutants are measured on a scale of 0-500, from good to hazerdous. 

**What is AQI?**\
The AQI is a daily measurement of air pollution that attempts to quantify the health hazards associated with five of the six criteria pollutants.

*Lead is not included because it is impracticle to evaulate daily, since it requires longer collection and analysis times.*

**How the AQI is defined**
* The AQI is defined on a scale of 0-500, with 500 being the worst health outcome. 
* Because each pollutant requires different monitoring techniques, and is hazardous at differing levels, each pollutant is evaluated seperately, but values are standardized so that a score, for example 70, corrolates to a similar health risk across all pollutants.
* A default value of 100 corresponds to the limit for what is defined as acceptable short term exposure unless otherwise defined. 
* A value of 50 is what is defined as an acceptable level annually, unless defined as half of the short term level.

# What You Need to Know About the Criteria Pollutants

## Carbon Monoxide
**What is it?**  
Carbon monoxide is a colorless, odorless gas produced by burning fossil fuels. Common sources include cars, trucks, and other combustion engines.

**Health effects**  
Short-term exposure reduces the blood’s ability to carry oxygen to vital organs.

### Who is vulnerable?
- People with certain heart conditions  
  - May already have reduced oxygen delivery to the heart  
  - Exposure can cause myocardial ischemia (reduced oxygen to the heart), often leading to chest pain (angina), especially during exercise or stress  
- Other high-risk groups:
  - People with anemia  
  - Older adults  
  - Pregnant people  

---

## Lead
**What is it?**  
Lead is a toxic heavy metal historically used in gasoline. Today, major sources include industrial processes such as battery recycling, lead smelting, and some aviation fuels.

**Health effects**  
Lead exposure is associated with neurodevelopmental effects in children, including reduced IQ and increased risk of learning and behavioral difficulties.

### Who is vulnerable?
- Pregnant people  
  - Lead can cross the placenta and affect fetal development  
- Infants, children, and adolescents  
  - Interferes with brain and nervous system development  

---

## Ground-Level Ozone
**What is it?**  
Ground-level ozone is a gas formed when pollutants from vehicles, industry, and power plants react in sunlight. Some natural sources, such as trees and vegetation, can also contribute to ozone formation.

It is important to distinguish ground-level ozone (harmful to breathe) from stratospheric ozone, which protects the Earth from ultraviolet radiation.

**Health effects**  
Short-term exposure can irritate the respiratory system, causing coughing, wheezing, chest pain, and shortness of breath. It can also reduce lung function and exercise capacity and increase susceptibility to respiratory infections.

Long-term exposure may contribute to asthma development and cause lasting lung damage.

### Who is vulnerable?
- People with asthma or other respiratory conditions  
  - Can experience worsened symptoms and increased medication use  
- People who exercise outdoors frequently  
  - Increased breathing rate leads to higher exposure  

---

## Nitrogen Dioxide
**What is it?**  
Nitrogen dioxide is formed during high-temperature combustion when nitrogen and oxygen react in the air. Major sources include vehicles, power plants, and other fuel-burning engines.

**Health effects**  
Can cause respiratory irritation, including coughing, wheezing, and shortness of breath.

### Who is vulnerable?
- People with asthma and other lung conditions  
- Children and older adults  

---

## Sulfur Dioxide
**What is it?**  
Sulfur dioxide is produced by burning sulfur-containing fossil fuels such as coal and oil. Major sources include heavy industry, metal smelting, and power plants.

**Health effects**  
Can irritate the airways, causing coughing, wheezing, shortness of breath, chest tightness, and difficulty breathing.

### Who is vulnerable?
- People with asthma and other respiratory conditions  
  - Symptoms may worsen even at low exposure levels  

---

## Particle Pollution
**What is it?**  
The EPA defines two types of particle pollution:

### PM10 – Coarse Particles
Airborne particles with a diameter ≤ 10 µm and > 2.5 µm.

These particles are primarily produced by mechanical processes (construction, crushing), combustion (wildfires, residential burning), and resuspended dust from roads and soil.

### PM2.5 – Fine Particles
Particles with a diameter ≤ 2.5 µm.

These particles are mainly produced by combustion (vehicles, power plants, wood burning, industrial processes) and atmospheric chemical reactions involving gaseous pollutants.

---

### Health Effects (PM2.5)
While both PM10 and PM2.5 are harmful, PM2.5 poses a greater health risk because it can penetrate deep into the lungs and enter the bloodstream.

Exposure is associated with:
- Cardiovascular and respiratory disease aggravation  
- Reduced heart and lung function  
- Increased allergic and respiratory symptoms  

Long-term exposure is linked to:
- Low birth weight  
- Increased risk of infant mortality from respiratory causes  

---

### Who is vulnerable?
- Children and infants  
- Pregnant people  
- Older adults  
- Individuals with heart or lung conditions  
- People with lower socioeconomic status
